PSERS Background

The Public Schools Employee’s Retirement System (PSERS) is a pension fund each Pennsylvania public school district must pay into. PSERS is governed by the PSERS Board of Trustees, a state body that operates completely outside the jurisdiction of school districts. Each December the Board of Trustees certifies a contribution rate, the rate at which all Pennsylvania public school districts must contribute in the upcoming fiscal year.

You have probably heard of PSERS. Over the last several years it has gained a fair amount of coverage in the press, and for good reason. Due to what could be considered bad policy around the management of this pension fund stemming back to the early 2000’s, the pension is currently severely underfunded. As a result, contribution rates have sky-rocketed over the last several years in an effort to catch-up, with future rates projected to increase as well. The rates are published here: PSERS Employer Contribution Rates.

With all the negative press surrounding PSERS, combined with the difficulty the average tax payer would have in verifying the cost of PSERS on Chichester, PSERS has made for the perfect scapegoat for some of the Chichester School Board members. I hope to change that by educating taxpayers. The first order of business is showing how to calculate PSERS contribution amounts.

Total PSERS Contribution

The Total PSERS Contribution Amount is the entire amount the district pays into PSERS which includes a very, very generous helping of state money. In Chichester's case the state subsidizes more than half the cost. The Total Contribution is determined by the Total Contribution rate, which for fiscal year 2019-2020 is set at 34.29%.

What this means, is that each district must pay into PSERS the total sum of all district salaries multiplied by the rate of 34.29%. For Chichester FY2019-2020, this works out to a Total Contribution Amount just shy of $11 million:

Net PSERS Contribution Amount - What Districts Actually Pay

If the above rates and amounts sound terrible, that is because we haven’t covered state reimbursements. Fortunately, there must be some kind of commonsense at play in the commonwealth. Instead of leaving it to school districts and their local tax bases to absorb these high contribution rates, the state alleviates most of the burden through state reimbursement moneys. These are reflected in PDE 2028 budgets as revenue under ‘7820 State Share of Retirement Contributions’. Budget revenues can be viewed here.

Coming Up Next...

As you can see, the net PSERS cost to the district has been decreasing over the last two years, and represents only 10% of spending increases over the last four years. That might come as a surprise. If you had attended last year's 5-22-19 board meeting, then you would certainly have walked away believing that the multi-million dollar deficit was due to PSERS increases. In the distorted portrait of district finances that the board painted that evening, it would have also seemed reasonable that a fund named after PSERS was used to balance that multi-million dollar deficit.
